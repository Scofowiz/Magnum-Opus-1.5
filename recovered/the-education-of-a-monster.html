The alley reeked of opium smoke and spoiled cabbage, a stink so thick Madeline tasted it on the back of her tongue. Lanterns glowed behind paper shutters overhead, painting the fog the color of old bruises. Somewhere a woman laughed—shrill, broken, already dead.

Liesel stepped from the shadow as if she owned it. “Choose,” she murmured, gloved hand resting on Madeline’s spine just above the corset. “The city is a menu. Learn to read.”

Madeline’s new sight sorted the darkness into useful pieces: a sailor slumped against a rain barrel, pulse flickering at his throat like a trapped moth; a prostitute coughing red into a handkerchief; a boy in a man’s coat licking opium from the tip of a long nail. Heartbeats overlapped—thirty-seven a minute, forty-three, a fragile twenty-nine. She smelled salt pork on the sailor’s breath, mercury ointment on the girl’s skin, the sour copper of the boy’s infected gums.

“Why not him?” Madeline whispered, nodding toward the sailor. Hunger cramped her stomach, a polite cramp, as though her corset had tightened another inch.

“Because his ship sails tomorrow and he’ll be missed.” Liesel’s voice stayed gentle, instructive. “We do not leave mysteries. A sailor disappears, the company sends men with lanterns. A whore dies, the precinct shrugs.”

The prostitute coughed again, speckling the fog with scarlet. Madeline’s gums throbbed where new teeth slid longer each night, delicate blades behind the old. One more drop, the hunger promised. One more and you’ll stop shaking.

Liesel glided forward. The woman looked up, eyes dull. A word passed—too soft to catch—and the prostitute offered her wrist as if greeting a long-lost friend. The vein showed purple beneath rice-paper skin.

“Watch,” Liesel said. She pressed coral lips to the vein, not kissing, simply drinking. The woman’s knees buckled; Liesel supported her weight with one arm, graceful as a dancer. Three heartbeats, four. She lifted her mouth, licked once to seal the wound, then eased the prostitute onto a doorstep. “Enough to weaken, not to kill. By morning she’ll think a customer roughed her.”

Madeline tasted the air—no death scent, only retreating life. Practical. Neat. She thought of Edward’s gambling ledgers: numbers arranged to look innocent while they bled you dry. This was cleaner.

“Your turn,” Liesel said. The sailor had begun to snore. They crossed to him; Liesel lifted his collar away with two fingers, exposing the throat. “Feel the rhythm. Match it.”

Madeline knelt. The man’s stubble rasped her palm; his pulse drummed against her lips when she bent. She bit, careful, the new incisors sliding through skin the way silk parts under scissors. Blood welled—hot, metallic, spiced with rum. She swallowed once, twice, counting. At twenty the edges of her vision brightened, colors blooming obscene. Thirty. Thirty-five. The sailor sighed, shifting.

She stopped. Tongue pressed to the punctures, she tasted her own thin saliva sealing the skin, the faint tang of iodine Liesel had made her carry. “Enough?”

“Enough,” Liesel confirmed. “Stand before you want more. The hunger lies; it always calls another drop harmless.”

Madeline straightened, light-headed, pleasantly hollow. The alley tilted, then steadied. Somewhere down the corridor of shacks, firecrackers cracked like distant gunshots. Tong wars, the papers called them—convenient explosions that explained away bodies.

They left the sailor snoring, the prostitute drowsing. At the alley mouth Liesel paused, brushing a fleck of blood from Madeline’s lip. “Lesson the second: never take from the same place twice. Return and they remember the dream of you.”

Madeline glanced back. The woman coughed again, weaker, but she lived. For the first time since Edward’s funeral, Madeline felt something like competence. It tasted of rust and smoke, but it was hers.

“Tomorrow,” Liesel said, “we hunt Nob Hill. Rich men bruise as easily as poor ones; they simply hide it under cravats.” She smiled, teeth bright in the fog. “Welcome to the endless dinner, Madeline. Pray you never lose your seat at the table.”

They walked back through Chinatown’s narrow throat, the fog parting like theater curtains. Paper lanterns swung overhead, bleeding red light onto the cobbles. Madeline’s new boots—Liesel’s gift, kid leather soft as sin—made no sound. She catalogued the rhythm of her own heart: slower now, sixty beats a minute, the pulse of something conserving itself.

At Dupont Street they joined the late-night parade: gamblers in sleeve garters, pipe carriers balancing wooden crates of opium, a priest in collar askew. No one glanced twice at two widows in black. Respectability was its own veil.

Liesel halted beneath a gas lamp. “Lesson the third,” she said, voice pitched for Madeline’s ear alone. “We purchase protection the way mortals buy bread.” She slipped a gold eagle into the hand of a passing constable; the man touched his helm, eyes sliding past them as if they were empty air. Transaction complete, covenant sealed.

Madeline felt the coin’s absence in her pocket—her last. “I thought immortals feared nothing.”

“Only hunger and boredom,” Liesel answered. “And the second is cured by the first.”

They turned onto Stockton, climbing toward the respectable glow of California Street. The smell changed—less opium, more horse dung and baker’s yeast. Here the fog carried the clang of cable-car bells instead of firecrackers. Civilization, Madeline thought, was merely a different flavor of savagery.

Halfway up the hill Liesel paused beside a recessed doorway. From her reticule she drew a small mirror backed in mother-of-pearl. “Hold it,” she ordered.

Madeline flinched. Mirrors were forbidden; the elixir’s pamphlet had been explicit. Yet curiosity outweighed caution. She looked.

The glass showed her face—familiar cheekbones, gray-threaded hair now darkened to wet coal, skin smoothed like porcelain under lacquer. Only the eyes startled: irises ringed in blood-shadow, pupils blown wide as if she stood in pitch. She did not appear younger so much as unfinished, clay that had forgotten its intended shape.

“See?” Liesel whispered. “We are not reflected; we are projected. What you glimpse is memory of yourself, not truth.” She snapped the mirror shut. “Carry it if you wish. Most of us learn to avoid accidental glimpses.”

Madeline slipped it into her sleeve pocket, beside the vial of iodine. Another tool, another tether to Liesel’s world.

At the crest they hailed a closed hack. Inside, velvet seats smelled of cedar and previous passengers—perfume, pipe tobacco, a faint metallic undercurrent she now recognized as old blood. She wondered whose. The driver cracked his whip; the vehicle lurched toward Russian Hill.

Madeline watched the city scroll past: wooden sidewalks, iron shutters, brothels masquerading as boarding houses. Everywhere, people hurried home before the fog thickened into curfew. She felt them—their pulses tapping against glass, their warmth leaking through cracks. She pressed her gloved hands together to keep them still.

Liesel observed her in silence, satisfaction tucked in the corner of her mouth. After a mile she spoke. “You fed tonight without killing. Well done.”

Madeline inclined her head. Praise from Liesel arrived like winter roses: beautiful, cold, thorned.

“Yet you tremble,” Liesel added. “Guilt?”

“Anticipation,” Madeline corrected. The word tasted honest. Guilt required a moral compass; hers had spun like a broken needle the moment she swallowed the elixir. What remained was simpler: hunger, and the knowledge that tomorrow she would need to feed again.

The hack rattled over cobblestones, each jolt sending a tremor through Madeline’s teeth. Not pain—never pain anymore—but awareness. The ivory felt longer tonight, pressing against her lower lip when she relaxed her jaw. She touched them with her tongue, counting: two lengthened incisors, sharp as embroidery needles. How many feeds before the others followed? How many before she could no longer pass for mortal even in lamplight?

Russian Hill resolved into quiet mansions, their windows dark respectable eyes. Liesel tapped the roof; the driver reined in beside a wrought-iron gate twined with dead ivy. “We walk from here,” she said. The fog was thinner, merely a veil instead of a shroud, yet Madeline’s boots whispered as though the ground itself listened.

They passed a small Catholic chapel—brick, one bell tower, wooden doors blistered by salt air. Liesel paused. “Saint Gabriel’s. The priests leave a key beneath the third brick. If ever you need sanctuary from our kind, go inside and stay off the altar. Consecrated ground binds more than it saves.”

Madeline filed the knowledge away, another coin in an invisible purse. She had learned to survive by cataloguing small mercies: the taste of blood, the weight of gold, the location of keys. Details kept one alive; grand philosophies merely decorated the trap.

At the corner of Hyde and Chestnut stood their destination: a narrow boarding house indistinguishable from its neighbors except for the brass plaque beside the door—an ouroboros coiled around the letter L. Liesel produced a key. Inside, the foyer smelled of beeswax and camphor, the scent of preservation. Gas-jets hissed behind etched globes, turning the wallpaper amber.

A manservant waited—tall, hair powdered white, eyes reflecting the flame without blinking. “The others have arrived,” he informed Liesel. “They wait in the solar.”

Madeline’s pulse quickened. She had heard mention of others—immortals older than California itself—but never met them. Tonight, apparently, the curriculum widened.

The solar occupied the top floor, windows facing south toward the bay. Moonlight silvered the room, revealing four figures arranged like chess pieces around a low table. A fifth chair stood empty; Liesel gestured Madeline into it.

Introductions flowed, each name landing with ceremonial weight.

Marcus—slight, dark, coat cut from midnight velvet—lifted two fingers in greeting. Where his hand passed through lamplight, shadow folded after it like cloth. “Shadowmancer,” he said, voice soft as soot. “I travel where light fears to linger.”

Beside him, identical women sat shoulder-to-shoulder, black hair threaded with jade combs. “Zhang Mei.” “Zhang Lin.” They spoke in perfect unison, then smiled as though sharing a private joke. “One mind,” they explained, “two mouths. We remember everything twice.”

Across the table lounged Cornelius—corpulent, pomaded, fingers heavy with rings. Rats threaded between his boots, fur glossy, eyes intelligent. He scratched one under its chin; it chittered, then stilled. “My children,” he murmured. “They go where I cannot, hear what I need. Cities are built on their backs.”

Liesel remained standing, hands resting on Madeline’s shoulders. “And you know my gift,” she said. “I speak; mortals believe. Convenient for hunting, essential for peace.”

Madeline felt their attention settle on her, measuring breath, heartbeat, the tremor of new teeth. She straightened. “I see endings,” she said, though until this moment she had not known it. The words arrived unbidden, certain. “When I touch skin, I know the hour and manner of death.”

Silence pooled, thick as mercury. Then Cornelius chuckled, low and wet. “How morbidly useful.”

Liesel’s fingers tightened—approval or warning, Madeline could not tell. “We share tonight so each understands the others’ strengths. Among immortals, knowledge is the only currency that never inflates.”

Marcus leaned forward; his chair made no sound, as though even furniture feared disturbing his shadow. “And among immortals, prophecy is a blade without handle. Be careful where you point it, Mrs. Ashton.”

Madeline met his gaze, saw her own reflection swallowed by darkness. She thought of the sailor breathing somewhere in Chinatown, of the prostitute wiping blood from her chin, of hunger pacing inside her like a caged cat. Blade without handle. Perhaps. But even a blunt weapon breaks skin if pressed hard enough.

Outside, the city tolled two o’clock—iron bells carried on fog. Liesel exhaled, satisfied. “Good. Now we hunt together, and learn how our gifts may serve the whole. The night is young, and San Francisco offers such variety.”

They left the house in pairs, drifting down the hill like ink diluted in water. Liesel and Madeline walked last, heels clicking a counter-rhythm to the distant foghorns. The city below was a bowl of spilled pearls—gaslight, moonlight, the occasional red bloom of a signal flare over the bay. Every halo looked edible.

At Bush Street they split. Cornelius vanished with his rats into a sewer grate; the Zhang twins melted toward the waterfront, heads tilted as though listening to the same private song; Marcus became a ripple along the shop-front shadows and was simply not there. Madeline and Liesel followed the cable rails west, toward the tenderloin district where music spilled from saloons at all hours.

The lesson, Liesel murmured, was to hunt in plain sight. “Society believes widows are harmless. Be the illusion they cherish.”

They paused beneath a theatre awning still gaudy with gilt posters. A senator’s carriage stood at the curb, horses steaming. The man himself descended, white gloves pristine despite the hour. He was florid, diamond stick-pin flashing, laughter booming off the velvet-lined lobby doors. When he noticed the two women in mourning, he swept off his top-hat.

“Ladies, permit an old escort inside. The opera is tedious without charming company.”

Liesel’s gloved hand brushed Madeline’s wrist—permission or command. Madeline accepted the senator’s arm. Skin touched skin above the cuff.

The vision struck like scalding tea thrown in her face:

Three weeks hence, a private dining room at the Occidental. Candle-flame doubled in polished silver. The senator laughing, mouth full of sole meunière. A bone sliding sideways, lodging. His eyes widening, fist pounding the white tablecloth. Wine goblet toppling—claret blooming like carnations across linen. The matron beside him screaming. Then silence, the meaty rattle of last breath. Date: 17th November, eleven twenty-two p.m., moon in the last quarter.

Madeline’s knees locked. The lobby’s gas-jets flared too bright; the gilt proscenium seemed to yawn. She managed to release his arm before the rest of the scene could finish unspooling.

“Forgive me,” she murmured, stepping back. “A sudden dizziness.”

The senator’s smile was all concern. “The fog plays tricks. Allow me to fetch champagne.”

Liesel interceded with practiced grace. “My companion is weary. Another evening, Senator.” She guided Madeline away, fingers cool through layers of bombazine.

Outside, the cold felt merciful. Madeline leaned against a hitching post, swallowing the iron taste of prophecy. Somewhere inside the theatre the senator would retake his seat, unaware that each note of the overture carried him closer to asphyxiation on a fishbone.

“Tell me,” Liesel said quietly.

Madeline did, voice trembling. When she finished, Liesel regarded the arched entrance where the senator had disappeared. “A useful warning—if he would listen. But he will not. Mortals treat fate as negotiable until the noose tightens.” She brushed a strand of hair from Madeline’s cheek. “Remember: we do not save; we select. Your gift is not mercy—it is intelligence.”

They moved on, past doormen who tipped their visors, past drunkards arguing over lottery tickets. At each corner Madeline felt the city’s pulse quicken, a thousand heartbeats drumming against her teeth. She wondered which belonged to the next sanctioned meal, which to tomorrow’s headlines, which to the secret ledger of her own growing hunger.

Near midnight they hailed another hack and returned to the house on Hyde Street. The manservant admitted them without a word, taking their cloaks as though they had never left. In the solar, fresh tea steamed beside crystal glasses of something darker. The others were already back—Marcus lounging in a puddle of lamplight, the twins murmuring numbers in Cantonese, Cornelius feeding his rats scraps of ham from a silver plate.

Madeline accepted a glass. The liquid inside was viscous, room temperature, scented with jasmine and copper. Not human blood—some subtler vintage, spiced and fortified. She sipped. Strength uncoiled along her veins like smoke rising through floorboards.

Across the table, Liesel lifted her own glass in silent toast. “To new talents,” she said, eyes gleaming. “May they serve us all.”

Madeline drank, tasting futures she could not yet name.

The journal began that same night, though Madeline did not yet trust paper to hold what she had seen. She sat at the escritoire in her borrowed room—third floor, rear window overlooking a kitchen garden where moonlit scarecrows stood guard over winter cabbages. Ink cooled in the well while she listened to the house settle: rafters creaking like old lungs, the manservant’s measured tread on the stair, the faint scratch of rats behind wainscot where Cornelius had released them for safekeeping.

She wrote the date first—October 27, 1876—then hesitated. Below it she set down only: Senator Henry D. Wilson, sole meunière, 17 Nov, 11:22 p.m. She underlined the hour twice, as though emphasis might keep destiny polite.

Footsteps paused outside her door. Breath held. Three taps—Liesel’s rhythm. Madeline opened to find her mentor draped in a peacock silk robe, candle in hand. The flame painted her face with fox-colored shadows.

“You are awake,” Liesel observed, gliding in without invitation. “Good. The others convene downstairs. We keep a small ceremony for first visions.” She set the candle beside the inkpot, watched the senator’s doom reflected upside-down in the well. “You will grow accustomed to carrying futures in your purse. Eventually you forget they are there.”

Madeline touched the page. “What if I spoke? Sent an anonymous note?”

Liesel’s smile curved, patient. “He would dine earlier, order beef instead. The bone would find him in soup, in a child’s kitchen, in the throat of a wife he kisses good-bye. Fate is a bored actress; change her costume and she still delivers the same final line.” She brushed the dried ink with a fingertip. “Better to study, to learn the shape of prophecy. Tonight you witnessed a single thread. Soon you will see the whole tapestry.”

From the pocket of her robe she produced a slim book bound in garnet leather. “Your ledger. Record time, circumstance, witness. Patterns emerge—rivers of repetition, eddies where death clusters.” She placed it beside the unfinished entry. “And never sign your name.”

Madeline accepted the weight of it, the cool leather drinking candle-warmth. “Have you ever altered one?”

“Once.” Liesel’s gaze went distant, fixed on the scarecrow visible through the window. “I loved him, so I warned. He fled the city, took a stagecoach north. Avalanche at Donner Pass buried the road. They found his hand still clutching my letter.” She turned back, voice soft. “Interference is simply another route to the same grave.”

A bell chimed below—three low notes that seemed to rise through the boards like damp. Liesel extended her hand. “Come. The twins wish to show you how they share death between them. Marcus has brought a condemned man from the county jail—murderer of shopkeepers, already sentenced. A perfect classroom.”

Madeline rose, closing the journal. She slipped it beneath her pillow, beside the mother-of-pearl mirror and the vial of iodine. Tools, warnings, toys—she no longer distinguished categories.

In the corridor the gaslights hissed lower, conserving coin. Shadows stretched long enough to meet Marcus where he waited at the stairhead, half his face lost to darkness. He inclined his head. “Mrs. Ashton. I have borrowed you a life ending tonight. Consider it demonstration.”

Madeline’s new teeth pricked her lower lip. She tasted her own blood, thin and bright. “Lead,” she said, voice steady. “I would learn how not to interfere.”

They descended single file, candlelight flickering across wallpaper patterned with peacocks whose eyes, in the shifting glow, seemed to follow the hem of her gown.

The cellar smelled of river-stone and rusted iron, a damp chill that settled in Madeline’s bones despite her new immunity to such mortal discomforts. A single lamp hung from a ceiling beam, throwing long shadows across the packed-earth floor. In the center sat a wooden chair, its arms scarred by previous lessons. The condemned man slumped there now, wrists bound with hemp rope, head lolling as though drunk on laudanum.

Marcus circled him like a cat considering a dying bird. “Observe,” he said, voice barely above a whisper. Yet the shadows themselves seemed to carry his words, echoing them back from corners where no light reached. “Death approaches, but slowly. We have time to study.”

The man lifted his head. Eyes bloodshot, cheeks hollow, but awareness sharp as broken glass. “You’re not the priest,” he rasped. “They said a priest would come.”

Liesel stepped from behind Madeline, movements fluid as mercury. “Priests serve different hungers,” she said. “We require confession of a different sort.”

From the far wall, the Zhang twins emerged as one organism, their silk slippers making no sound on the dirt. They carried between them a small lacquered box, which they set upon a low stool. When they opened it, Madeline caught the scent of something medicinal—alcohol, perhaps, or the bitter herb doctors used to clean wounds.

Cornelius lounged against the wall, rats clustering around his boots like courtiers. One climbed his sleeve, perched on his shoulder, tiny paws grooming his collar. “Tell us your story, Jeremiah,” he cooed. “Tell us how you killed them.”

The condemned man—Jeremiah—shuddered. “They wouldn’t give me the money. Just a few dollars, enough for food. My Mary was sick, the children crying…” His voice cracked. “I didn’t mean to hurt anyone.”

“But you did hurt them,” Liesel said, gentle as a mother coaxing a child. “Three shopkeepers with families of their own. You took what was theirs, then took their lives when they protested.” She moved behind him, hands resting on his shoulders. “The court was merciful. They sentenced you to hang. We are not so merciful.”

Madeline watched the man’s pulse flutter at his throat, counting the beats. Seventy-two a minute, elevated but steady. Not yet afraid, not truly. Still clinging to the illusion of human mercy.

Liesel caught her eye. “Would you like to see his death, Madeline? Touch him, and know if the rope will break his neck or merely strangle him slowly.”

She shook her head. “I already know.” The words surprised her, emerging with quiet certainty. “He dies in seventeen days, not on the gallows. Fever in the prison infirmary. They’ll bury him in the pauper’s field beyond the mission.”

Jeremiah’s head snapped up. “Liar! They’re hanging me tomorrow!”

“No,” Marcus said, and the shadows around him deepened, swallowing more of the lamplight. “They will delay. A paperwork error, a missing signature. Seventeen days of waiting, wondering if mercy might yet arrive. Then the fever, the delirium, the slow surrender.” He smiled, teeth white against the darkness. “We merely hasten what already waits.”

The Zhang twins opened their box fully now, revealing a collection of instruments: thin needles, small knives, curved hooks that caught the lamplight. They selected one needle each, holding them up like conductors preparing an orchestra.

“Watch,” they said in unison, and approached Jeremiah from either side. “Death is a duet. Listen for the harmony.”
